The need

We own more Bibles than ever. We just don't read them.

1 in 7
Half of Americans own a Bible — but only a sliver read it regularly.
<10%
Fewer than one in ten could name the first five books of the Bible.
25%
A quarter of churchgoers thought the Golden Rule was one of the Ten Commandments.

And yet people aren't indifferent. More than half of Americans say they wish they read the Bible more. They sense there's something worth finding — they just don't know where to start, or whether they'd understand it if they did.

But something's changing

The hunger is coming back.

Weekly Bible reading just jumped to 42 percent of American adults — a twelve-point leap in a single year, led by young adults. The question is what happens next. When someone finally opens the Book, will anyone help them make sense of it?

It's an old problem. In Acts, a court official from Ethiopia rides home reading the prophet Isaiah. When Philip asks if he understands it, the man gives one of the most honest answers in all of Scripture:

So Philip climbed up and sat beside him — and by the end of that road, everything had changed. That's the gap we exist to fill.

Why we're here

Helping you know the Word — and live it.

You really can understand the Bible yourself. It wasn't written for scholars in ivory towers; it was written for shepherds, fishermen, and ordinary people living ordinary lives. So we won't just hand you a list of things to believe — we'll teach you how to dig: how to read closely, ask good questions, and reach convictions that are truly your own.

Because knowing the Word is only the start. The whole point is to live it.
